## Section 1: Pharmacology & Clinical Pharmacy

### Q1. Theophylline is an older but highly effective bronchodilator used in the management of chronic asthma and COPD. What is its primary biochemical mechanism of action?
A) Stimulation of Beta-2 adrenergic receptors
B) Inhibition of Phosphodiesterase (PDE) enzyme
C) Blockade of Leukotriene receptors
D) Inhibition of Mast cell degranulation

**Correct Answer:** B) Inhibition of Phosphodiesterase (PDE) enzyme
**Explanation:** Theophylline is a methylxanthine derivative (similar to caffeine). It induces bronchodilation primarily by competitively inhibiting the enzyme Phosphodiesterase (specifically PDE-III and PDE-IV). By inhibiting this enzyme, it prevents the breakdown of intracellular cyclic AMP (cAMP). High levels of cAMP in bronchial smooth muscle lead to profound, sustained relaxation and bronchodilation. It also acts as a competitive antagonist at adenosine receptors.

### Q2. Acarbose and Miglitol are oral antidiabetic medications utilized to manage Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus. They exert their therapeutic effect by:
A) Stimulating insulin release from pancreatic beta cells
B) Increasing peripheral insulin sensitivity
C) Inhibiting alpha-glucosidase enzymes in the intestinal brush border
D) Promoting glucose excretion in the urine

**Correct Answer:** C) Inhibiting alpha-glucosidase enzymes in the intestinal brush border
**Explanation:** Acarbose and Miglitol are competitive, reversible inhibitors of alpha-glucosidase, the enzyme located in the brush border of the small intestine that is responsible for breaking down complex carbohydrates into simple monosaccharides (glucose). By blocking this enzyme, these drugs significantly delay the digestion and absorption of carbohydrates, effectively flattening the postprandial (post-meal) spike in blood glucose levels. 

### Q3. Patients on chronic, high-dose NSAID therapy (like Indomethacin) are at a high risk of developing severe gastric ulcers. Which synthetic prostaglandin analogue is specifically co-administered to prevent NSAID-induced peptic ulcers?
A) Misoprostol
B) Alprostadil
C) Dinoprostone
D) Latanoprost

**Correct Answer:** A) Misoprostol
**Explanation:** NSAIDs cause ulcers by blocking the COX-1 enzyme, drastically reducing the production of protective gastric prostaglandins. Misoprostol is a synthetic analogue of Prostaglandin E1 (PGE1). When administered orally, it acts directly on gastric parietal cells to inhibit acid secretion and aggressively stimulates the secretion of protective gastric mucus and bicarbonate. It is absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy as it can induce uterine contractions and abortion.

### Q4. Primaquine is a highly effective antimalarial drug, specifically used for the radical cure of Plasmodium vivax and ovale. However, it can cause severe, life-threatening hemolytic anemia in patients with a genetic deficiency of which specific enzyme?
A) Hexokinase
B) Glucose-6-Phosphate Dehydrogenase (G6PD)
C) Pyruvate Kinase
D) UDP-Glucuronosyltransferase

**Correct Answer:** B) Glucose-6-Phosphate Dehydrogenase (G6PD)
**Explanation:** G6PD is an essential enzyme that protects red blood cells from oxidative stress by regenerating glutathione. In patients with an inherited X-linked G6PD deficiency, exposure to strong oxidizing drugs like Primaquine, Sulfonamides, or Dapsone overwhelms the red blood cells' defense mechanisms. This causes the hemoglobin to denature (forming Heinz bodies) and leads to massive, potentially fatal hemolysis (rupturing of red blood cells).

### Q5. Amphotericin B is a powerful, broad-spectrum, intravenously administered antifungal agent used for life-threatening systemic fungal infections. Its most notorious and dose-limiting adverse effect is:
A) Hepatotoxicity
B) Cardiotoxicity
C) Nephrotoxicity
D) Ototoxicity

**Correct Answer:** C) Nephrotoxicity
**Explanation:** Amphotericin B (often grimly nicknamed "Ampho-terrible" by clinicians) works by binding to ergosterol and tearing massive pores in the fungal cell membrane. Unfortunately, it also binds somewhat to human cholesterol, making it highly toxic. Its primary dose-limiting toxicity is severe, often irreversible Nephrotoxicity (kidney damage). It causes a massive drop in glomerular filtration rate (GFR) and severe wasting of potassium and magnesium in the urine. 

### Q6. Succinylcholine is a unique neuromuscular blocking agent used to facilitate rapid endotracheal intubation. Unlike non-depolarizing blockers (like Pancuronium), Succinylcholine is metabolized entirely by which enzyme in the blood plasma?
A) Acetylcholinesterase
B) Pseudocholinesterase (Butyrylcholinesterase)
C) Monoamine Oxidase
D) Cytochrome P450 3A4

**Correct Answer:** B) Pseudocholinesterase (Butyrylcholinesterase)
**Explanation:** Succinylcholine is a depolarizing neuromuscular blocker. It causes initial muscle fasciculations followed by flaccid paralysis. It has an incredibly rapid onset and an ultra-short duration of action (about 5-10 minutes) because it is rapidly hydrolyzed in the bloodstream by Pseudocholinesterase (also known as plasma cholinesterase). Patients with a genetic deficiency or atypical form of this enzyme will experience prolonged, life-threatening apnea after receiving the drug.

### Q7. Zidovudine (AZT) was the very first antiretroviral drug approved for the treatment of HIV/AIDS. Pharmacologically, it belongs to which class of drugs?
A) Protease Inhibitors (PIs)
B) Non-Nucleoside Reverse Transcriptase Inhibitors (NNRTIs)
C) Nucleoside Reverse Transcriptase Inhibitors (NRTIs)
D) Integrase Strand Transfer Inhibitors (INSTIs)

**Correct Answer:** C) Nucleoside Reverse Transcriptase Inhibitors (NRTIs)
**Explanation:** Zidovudine is a thymidine analogue. It belongs to the Nucleoside Reverse Transcriptase Inhibitor (NRTI) class. Once inside the host cell, it is phosphorylated into an active triphosphate form. The HIV reverse transcriptase enzyme mistakenly incorporates this drug into the growing viral DNA chain. Because Zidovudine lacks a 3'-OH group, it causes immediate DNA chain termination, halting viral replication. Its major toxicity is severe bone marrow suppression.

### Q8. Acetazolamide is a mild diuretic frequently utilized for the treatment of high altitude sickness and open-angle glaucoma. At which specific anatomical site in the nephron does it exert its primary pharmacological action?
A) Thick ascending limb of the Loop of Henle
B) Distal convoluted tubule
C) Collecting duct
D) Proximal convoluted tubule

**Correct Answer:** D) Proximal convoluted tubule
**Explanation:** Acetazolamide is a Carbonic Anhydrase (CA) inhibitor. It acts almost exclusively in the proximal convoluted tubule (PCT) of the kidney. By inhibiting carbonic anhydrase, it drastically reduces the reabsorption of bicarbonate (HCO3-) and sodium. This leads to mild diuresis and the alkalinization of urine, while causing a mild systemic hyperchloremic metabolic acidosis. This acidosis stimulates breathing, which is why it helps prevent high altitude pulmonary edema (HAPE).

### Q9. Valproic acid is a broad-spectrum antiepileptic drug that is strictly avoided during pregnancy whenever possible because it carries a highly elevated risk of causing which specific severe congenital malformation?
A) Cleft lip and palate
B) Neural tube defects (e.g., Spina bifida)
C) Congenital heart defects (e.g., Ebstein's anomaly)
D) Phocomelia (Limb deformities)

**Correct Answer:** B) Neural tube defects (e.g., Spina bifida)
**Explanation:** Valproic acid (Sodium Valproate) is a known and highly potent teratogen. Exposure during the first trimester of pregnancy significantly increases the risk of Neural Tube Defects (NTDs), specifically spina bifida and anencephaly. This is believed to be due to its interference with folate metabolism. If a woman of childbearing age must take Valproic acid, she must be supplemented with high-dose folic acid prior to conception.

### Q10. Parathion and Malathion are agricultural organophosphate insecticides that can cause severe, fatal poisoning in humans. They exert their profound toxicity by:
A) Competitively blocking muscarinic receptors
B) Irreversibly inhibiting acetylcholinesterase
C) Depleting dopamine stores in the CNS
D) Directly stimulating nicotinic receptors

**Correct Answer:** B) Irreversibly inhibiting acetylcholinesterase
**Explanation:** Organophosphates are highly toxic nerve agents. They bind covalently and irreversibly to the active site of the enzyme Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) via a process called phosphorylation. Without AChE to break it down, acetylcholine builds up massively in the synaptic clefts of the central nervous system, parasympathetic nerve endings, and neuromuscular junctions, leading to a fatal cholinergic crisis (SLUDGE syndrome and respiratory paralysis).

## Section 2: Pharmaceutics & Engineering

### Q11. In solid dosage form manufacturing, Lactose is widely used as a diluent. However, if lactose is formulated with a drug containing a primary amine group (like Amphetamine), it causes the tablet to turn brown over time. This classic incompatibility is known as:
A) The Fenton Reaction
B) The Maillard Reaction
C) The Cannizzaro Reaction
D) Polymorphic transformation

**Correct Answer:** B) The Maillard Reaction
**Explanation:** The Maillard Reaction is a classic, frequently tested chemical incompatibility in pharmaceutics. It is a non-enzymatic browning reaction that occurs strictly between reducing sugars (like lactose, glucose, and maltose) and compounds containing a primary amine group (or amino acids). Over time, especially in the presence of moisture or alkaline lubricants like magnesium stearate, the mixture turns a brownish-yellow, indicating degradation of the active drug.

### Q12. Fluid Energy Mills (also known as Jet Mills) are utilized in the pharmaceutical industry to achieve ultra-fine particle size reduction (micronization). What is the fundamental physical principle of size reduction operating in this mill?
A) Cutting and shearing
B) Pure compression
C) Impact and attrition via high-velocity air streams
D) Grinding between two heavy rotating stones

**Correct Answer:** C) Impact and attrition via high-velocity air streams
**Explanation:** The Fluid Energy Mill contains absolutely no moving mechanical parts. Instead, high-pressure, high-velocity streams of air or inert gas are injected into the grinding chamber. The turbulent air causes the drug particles to violently crash into each other (Attrition) and against the walls of the chamber (Impact). This results in ultra-fine micronization (1 to 20 micrometers), making it ideal for thermolabile drugs and inhalation aerosols.

### Q13. Macrogols are frequently utilized in the formulation of pharmaceutical ointments and suppositories. Chemically, "Macrogols" are high molecular weight polymers of:
A) Polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP)
B) Polyethylene Glycol (PEG)
C) Cellulose acetate
D) Polymethyl methacrylate

**Correct Answer:** B) Polyethylene Glycol (PEG)
**Explanation:** Macrogol is the official, non-proprietary pharmacopeial name for Polyethylene Glycol (PEG). PEGs are highly versatile polymers whose physical state depends on their molecular weight (PEG 400 is a liquid, while PEG 4000 is a solid wax). They are highly hydrophilic, making them the absolute premier choice for formulating completely water-soluble ointment bases and water-soluble suppository bases that easily wash off the skin and do not melt, but rather dissolve in body fluids.

### Q14. In the commercial manufacturing of hard gelatin capsules, what specific pharmaceutical excipient is universally added to the molten gelatin mass to act as an opacifier?
A) Talc
B) Magnesium stearate
C) Titanium dioxide
D) Sodium benzoate

**Correct Answer:** C) Titanium dioxide
**Explanation:** Titanium dioxide (TiO2) is a brilliant white, inert pigment. It is universally added to the gelatin mixture during capsule manufacturing to act as an opacifier. It makes the capsule shell completely opaque, which serves two critical purposes: it protects highly photosensitive (light-sensitive) drugs inside the capsule from UV degradation, and it completely masks the potentially unpleasant appearance of the powder contents from the patient.

### Q15. In the compounding and formulation of suppositories, the "Displacement Value" of a specific drug is mathematically defined as:
A) The number of parts by weight of the drug that displaces exactly one part by weight of the suppository base
B) The number of parts by volume of base required to dissolve the drug
C) The melting point shift caused by adding the drug to the base
D) The time required for the suppository to melt in the rectum

**Correct Answer:** A) The number of parts by weight of the drug that displaces exactly one part by weight of the suppository base
**Explanation:** Suppository molds are calibrated by volume, not weight. Because drugs have different densities than the cocoa butter base, adding a heavy drug powder will change the total weight of the suppository while keeping the volume the same. The displacement value is essential to accurately calculate how much base must be physically removed to make room for the drug, ensuring the final molded suppository contains the exact requested dosage.

### Q16. According to standard pharmacopeial monographs, "Water for Injection" (WFI) utilized for bulk compounding of parenterals must strictly be free from:
A) All dissolved oxygen
B) Pyrogens (bacterial endotoxins)
C) All living microorganisms (completely sterile)
D) Isotonic agents

**Correct Answer:** B) Pyrogens (bacterial endotoxins)
**Explanation:** This is a classic trick question. "Water for Injection" (WFI) must be highly purified and absolutely free from pyrogens (endotoxins) because it is going into the bloodstream. However, it does *not* have to be completely sterile at the time of compounding. It will undergo terminal sterilization (like autoclaving) *after* the drug is dissolved and sealed in the ampoule. "Sterile Water for Injection," on the other hand, is pre-sterilized and used to reconstitute dry powders.

## Section 3: Pharmacognosy

### Q17. The Murexide test is a definitive, highly characteristic qualitative chemical test utilized exclusively for the identification of which class of alkaloids?
A) Tropane alkaloids
B) Purine alkaloids
C) Isoquinoline alkaloids
D) Indole alkaloids

**Correct Answer:** B) Purine alkaloids
**Explanation:** The Murexide test is the gold-standard identification test for Purine alkaloids, such as Caffeine (found in tea and coffee), Theophylline, and Theobromine (found in cocoa). The drug extract is treated with potassium chlorate and a drop of hydrochloric acid, evaporated to dryness, and exposed to ammonia vapor. A distinct, brilliant purplish-red color is produced, confirming the presence of the purine ring.

### Q18. Indian Podophyllum (Podophyllum hexandrum) is a highly valued medicinal resin. Its primary active chemical constituent, Podophyllotoxin, is utilized in modern medicine primarily for its:
A) Potent antimalarial properties
B) Cardiotonic activity
C) Antineoplastic (anticancer) and drastic purgative properties
D) Local anesthetic effects

**Correct Answer:** C) Antineoplastic (anticancer) and drastic purgative properties
**Explanation:** Podophyllum resin (Podophyllin) acts as a violent, drastic purgative if ingested. However, its immense modern medical value lies in Podophyllotoxin, which is an antimitotic agent. It arrests cell division by binding to tubulin and preventing microtubule formation. It is applied topically to treat genital warts, and its semi-synthetic derivatives (Etoposide and Teniposide) are crucial intravenous chemotherapeutic drugs used to treat testicular cancer and small cell lung cancer.

### Q19. Rauwolfia serpentina is a widely studied botanical drug. Which specific anatomical part of this plant is harvested, dried, and extracted to obtain the antihypertensive alkaloid Reserpine?
A) Leaves
B) Flowers
C) Dried roots and rhizomes
D) Seeds

**Correct Answer:** C) Dried roots and rhizomes
**Explanation:** The active indole alkaloids (Reserpine, Ajmaline, Deserpidine) are concentrated almost entirely in the underground parts of the plant. Therefore, the dried roots and rhizomes of Rauwolfia serpentina (family Apocynaceae) are the official, pharmacopeial crude drug. Harvesting the leaves or seeds would yield virtually zero medicinal value.

### Q20. In the quantitative microscopy and quality control of leaf drugs, the "Stomatal Index" provides a highly constant value to identify adulterants. How is the Stomatal Index mathematically defined?
A) The total number of stomata per square millimeter of the leaf epidermis
B) The ratio of the number of stomata to the total number of epidermal cells
C) The percentage proportion of the number of stomata to the total number of epidermal cells plus stomata
D) The physical size of the guard cells

**Correct Answer:** C) The percentage proportion of the number of stomata to the total number of epidermal cells plus stomata
**Explanation:** While the "Stomatal Number" (total stomata per mm²) varies greatly depending on the age of the leaf, environmental conditions, and soil, the Stomatal Index remains remarkably constant for a specific plant species. The formula is: SI = [S / (E + S)] × 100, where 'S' is the number of stomata and 'E' is the number of ordinary epidermal cells in the same microscopic field. It is a highly reliable parameter for standardization.

### Q21. Fiehe’s test is a rapid, standard chemical evaluation utilized in pharmacognosy specifically to detect the presence of artificial invert sugar (adulteration) in which natural product?
A) Castor oil
B) Beeswax
C) Honey
D) Wool fat (Lanolin)

**Correct Answer:** C) Honey
**Explanation:** Genuine, pure honey is frequently adulterated with cheap commercial invert sugar or high-fructose corn syrup to increase profits. During the commercial manufacturing of artificial invert sugar, sucrose is boiled with acid, which inevitably forms a byproduct called hydroxymethylfurfural (HMF). Fiehe’s test detects this HMF. When the honey is extracted with ether and treated with a solution of resorcinol in hydrochloric acid, a transient cherry-red color instantly proves the honey is adulterated.

## Section 4: Jurisprudence, Biochemistry & Anatomy

### Q22. Under the Indian Drugs and Cosmetics Rules, Schedule K occupies a unique legal position. It specifies:
A) The list of drugs exempted from the provisions relating to the manufacture of drugs
B) The list of drugs exempted from certain provisions relating to the import of drugs
C) The list of drugs exempted from certain provisions applicable to the manufacture and sale of drugs
D) The minimum requirements for running a retail pharmacy

**Correct Answer:** C) The list of drugs exempted from certain provisions applicable to the manufacture and sale of drugs
**Explanation:** Schedule K outlines specific exemptions from Chapter IV of the Act (which governs manufacture, sale, and distribution). For example, it allows a registered medical practitioner to dispense medicines to their own patients from their clinic without needing to obtain a formal retail pharmacy drug license. Similarly, it exempts certain household remedies from strict sale provisions.

### Q23. The Drugs and Cosmetics Act of 1940 is divided into schedules. "The First Schedule" of this Act specifically contains:
A) Forms for drug manufacturing licenses
B) Standards for biological products
C) The names of authoritative books of the Ayurvedic, Siddha, and Unani Tibb systems
D) The list of life-saving emergency drugs

**Correct Answer:** C) The names of authoritative books of the Ayurvedic, Siddha, and Unani Tibb systems
**Explanation:** Do not confuse the schedules of the *Act* with the schedules of the *Rules* (like Schedule M, H, X, etc.). The Drugs and Cosmetics Act itself has only two schedules. The First Schedule lists all the officially recognized, traditional authoritative textbooks (like Charaka Samhita, Sushruta Samhita) of Ayurvedic, Siddha, and Unani medicine. The Second Schedule lays down the standards to be complied with by imported drugs and drugs manufactured for sale.

### Q24. A patient suffering from severe malnutrition presents with Nyctalopia (night blindness) and severe drying of the conjunctiva (Xerophthalmia). This is the classic clinical presentation of a severe deficiency in:
A) Vitamin K
B) Vitamin B1 (Thiamine)
C) Vitamin A (Retinol)
D) Vitamin E (Tocopherol)

**Correct Answer:** C) Vitamin A (Retinol)
**Explanation:** Vitamin A is an essential fat-soluble vitamin. Retinal (the active aldehyde form) is a crucial structural component of rhodopsin, the light-sensitive pigment in the rod cells of the retina responsible for vision in low-light conditions. A deficiency leads to an inability to see in the dark (night blindness). Prolonged deficiency causes the tear ducts to dry up and the cornea to ulcerate (Xerophthalmia), leading to irreversible blindness.

### Q25. In the terminal stage of cellular respiration known as the Electron Transport Chain (ETC), what is the final, ultimate electron acceptor?
A) Carbon dioxide
B) Water
C) Molecular Oxygen (O2)
D) NAD+

**Correct Answer:** C) Molecular Oxygen (O2)
**Explanation:** In the mitochondria, high-energy electrons are passed down a chain of protein complexes (the ETC), pumping protons to create an electrochemical gradient that drives massive ATP synthesis. At the very end of this chain (Complex IV / Cytochrome c oxidase), the spent electrons are finally donated to molecular Oxygen (O2). The oxygen combines with these electrons and free protons (H+) to harmlessly form metabolic Water (H2O). If oxygen is absent, the entire chain halts, and the cell dies.

### Q26. Many digestive enzymes, such as Pepsin and Trypsin, are synthesized and secreted by the body in an inactive precursor form to prevent them from digesting the organs that produce them. These inactive enzyme precursors are scientifically termed:
A) Isoenzymes
B) Apoenzymes
C) Holoenzymes
D) Zymogens

**Correct Answer:** D) Zymogens
**Explanation:** Zymogens (or proenzymes) are biologically inactive proteins. They must undergo highly specific biochemical cleavage (usually the removal of a small masking peptide sequence) to reveal the active site and become functional. For example, the stomach secretes the zymogen Pepsinogen. When Pepsinogen contacts the harsh stomach acid (HCl), it is cleaved into the active, highly destructive enzyme Pepsin, which begins digesting dietary proteins.

### Q27. In the neuroanatomy of the human peripheral nervous system, what is the exact total number of pairs of spinal nerves that emerge from the spinal cord?
A) 12 pairs
B) 24 pairs
C) 31 pairs
D) 33 pairs

**Correct Answer:** C) 31 pairs
**Explanation:** While there are 12 pairs of cranial nerves that emerge directly from the brain, there are exactly 31 pairs of spinal nerves that emerge from the spinal cord to innervate the rest of the body. They are classified regionally: 8 Cervical pairs, 12 Thoracic pairs, 5 Lumbar pairs, 5 Sacral pairs, and 1 Coccygeal pair. Remember the difference between spinal nerves (31 pairs) and vertebrae (33 individual bones).

### Q28. Deep within the complex anatomy of the inner ear, the "Organ of Corti" is a highly specialized sensory structure explicitly responsible for the physiological transduction of:
A) Rotational balance
B) Linear acceleration
C) Hearing (Auditory signals)
D) Pain sensation

**Correct Answer:** C) Hearing (Auditory signals)
**Explanation:** The Organ of Corti is housed entirely within the spiral-shaped cochlea of the inner ear. It contains thousands of highly sensitive mechanoreceptor cells called "hair cells." When sound waves cause the basilar membrane to vibrate, these tiny hair cells are bent against the tectorial membrane above them. This physical bending opens ion channels, generating action potentials that travel via the auditory nerve (CN VIII) to the brain, allowing us to hear.

### Q29. White Blood Cells (Leukocytes) are divided into granulocytes and agranulocytes based on their microscopic appearance. Which two cell types are classified exclusively as Agranulocytes?
A) Neutrophils and Eosinophils
B) Lymphocytes and Monocytes
C) Basophils and Lymphocytes
D) Monocytes and Neutrophils

**Correct Answer:** B) Lymphocytes and Monocytes
**Explanation:** Leukocytes are the core of the immune system. Granulocytes possess highly visible, densely staining granules in their cytoplasm and include Neutrophils, Eosinophils, and Basophils. Agranulocytes lack these prominent cytoplasmic granules when viewed under a standard light microscope and consist solely of Lymphocytes (T-cells, B-cells) and Monocytes (which mature into tissue macrophages). 

### Q30. The human knee joint, which permits only uniaxial flexion and extension movements (bending and straightening) in a single anatomical plane, is a classic anatomical example of a:
A) Ball-and-socket joint
B) Pivot joint
C) Hinge joint
D) Gliding joint

**Correct Answer:** C) Hinge joint
**Explanation:** Synovial joints are classified by the movement they permit. The knee joint and the elbow joint are textbook examples of Hinge joints (Ginglymus). They operate exactly like the hinge on a door, allowing movement exclusively in one plane (flexion and extension). In contrast, the shoulder and hip are ball-and-socket joints, allowing a vast range of multi-axial movement including rotation.

## Special Bonus: Pharmacy Mnemonics

Lock down these critical pharmacology facts with these high-yield memory tricks!

### 1. Types of White Blood Cells (Order of abundance in blood)
**Mnemonic: Never Let Monkeys Eat Bananas**
From highest concentration to lowest:
* **N**eutrophils (60-70%)
* **L**ymphocytes (20-25%)
* **M**onocytes (3-8%)
* **E**osinophils (2-4%)
* **B**asophils (<1%)

### 2. Liver Enzyme Inducers (Cytochrome P450 Inducers)
**Mnemonic: CRAP GPS**
These drugs rev up the liver enzymes, causing other medications to be metabolized and destroyed much faster, lowering their effectiveness.
* **C**arbamazepine
* **R**ifampin
* **A**lcohol (Chronic abuse)
* **P**henytoin
* **G**riseofulvin
* **P**henobarbital
* **S**ulfonylureas

### 3. Causes of Microcytic Hypochromic Anemia
**Mnemonic: TAILS**
When red blood cells are small (microcytic) and pale (hypochromic), look for these causes:
* **T**halassemia
* **A**nemia of chronic disease
* **I**ron deficiency (The most common cause globally)
* **L**ead poisoning
* **S**ideroblastic anemia
