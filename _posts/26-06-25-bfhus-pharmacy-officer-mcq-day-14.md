## Section 1: Pharmacology & Clinical Pharmacy

### Q1. A 7-year-old child is diagnosed with "Absence Seizures" (Petit Mal), characterized by brief, sudden lapses of consciousness with a blank stare. Which antiepileptic drug is universally considered the absolute first-line treatment for this specific seizure type?
A) Phenytoin
B) Carbamazepine
C) Ethosuximide
D) Phenobarbital

**Correct Answer:** C) Ethosuximide
**Explanation:** Ethosuximide is the gold standard, first-line pharmacological agent exclusively used for the treatment of uncomplicated absence seizures. It works by specifically blocking T-type voltage-gated calcium channels in the thalamus, which are responsible for generating the 3-Hz spike-and-wave discharges characteristic of absence seizures. It is completely ineffective against generalized tonic-clonic or partial seizures. If the patient has mixed seizures (absence + tonic-clonic), Valproic Acid is the drug of choice.

### Q2. While Loop diuretics (like Furosemide) typically cause a massive loss of calcium in the urine (hypocalcemia), Thiazide diuretics (like Hydrochlorothiazide) have the exact opposite effect. Thiazides are clinically known to cause:
A) Hypocalcemia
B) Hypercalcemia
C) Hypernatremia
D) Hypouricemia

**Correct Answer:** B) Hypercalcemia
**Explanation:** This is a classic exam distinction. Thiazide diuretics block the Na+/Cl- symporter in the early distal convoluted tubule. By lowering intracellular sodium, they enhance the activity of the basolateral Na+/Ca2+ exchanger, which vigorously pulls calcium out of the urine and back into the blood. This results in decreased calcium excretion and subsequent Hypercalcemia. This calcium-saving property makes Thiazides incredibly useful in patients with osteoporosis or a history of calcium oxalate kidney stones.

### Q3. High-dose Methotrexate is frequently utilized in the chemotherapy of acute lymphoblastic leukemia. To prevent fatal, irreversible bone marrow suppression and mucosal damage to normal cells, which specific "rescue" agent is routinely administered 24 hours later?
A) N-acetylcysteine
B) Mesna
C) Leucovorin (Folinic Acid)
D) Dexrazoxane

**Correct Answer:** C) Leucovorin (Folinic Acid)
**Explanation:** Methotrexate competitively inhibits the enzyme dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR), depriving cells of active folate needed for DNA synthesis. Normal, healthy cells will also die without it. Leucovorin (Folinic acid) is a fully active derivative of folic acid that completely bypasses the blocked DHFR enzyme. It selectively "rescues" the normal, healthy cells from methotrexate toxicity without reversing the drug's antineoplastic effect on the tumor cells.

### Q4. Workers in explosives manufacturing factories exposed to daily nitrates historically experienced terrible headaches on Mondays that disappeared by Wednesday, only to return the following Monday. This classic "Monday Disease" demonstrates which pharmacological phenomenon?
A) Idiosyncrasy
B) Tachyphylaxis (Tolerance)
C) Synergism
D) Anaphylaxis

**Correct Answer:** B) Tachyphylaxis (Tolerance)
**Explanation:** Tachyphylaxis is the rapid, acute development of pharmacological tolerance. Nitrates (used as explosives and as anti-anginal drugs like Nitroglycerin) rapidly deplete the sulfhydryl (-SH) groups in vascular smooth muscle required to release nitric oxide. The workers developed tolerance over the workweek (headaches disappeared). Over the weekend, away from the factory, their sulfhydryl stores regenerated. Upon returning on Monday, the renewed intense vasodilation caused massive "Monday headaches."

### Q5. Beta-blockers are a massive class of drugs, but Nebivolol stands out from the rest. Alongside its highly selective Beta-1 blockade, Nebivolol uniquely lowers blood pressure by stimulating the release of which endogenous vasodilator?
A) Histamine
B) Bradykinin
C) Nitric Oxide (NO)
D) Prostacyclin

**Correct Answer:** C) Nitric Oxide (NO)
**Explanation:** Nebivolol is a highly unique, third-generation beta-blocker. It provides the most highly selective Beta-1 receptor blockade available, making it very cardioselective. Additionally, it exerts direct vasodilatory properties by stimulating endothelial Nitric Oxide Synthase (eNOS), releasing Nitric Oxide (NO). This dual mechanism results in excellent blood pressure reduction with a significantly lower risk of erectile dysfunction and fatigue compared to older beta-blockers like Propranolol or Atenolol.

### Q6. Acyclovir is a widely prescribed antiviral agent that is highly effective against Herpes Simplex (HSV) and Varicella-Zoster (VZV) viruses. However, Acyclovir is administered as a prodrug. It must be activated by which highly specific viral enzyme?
A) Viral Reverse Transcriptase
B) Viral Protease
C) Viral Thymidine Kinase
D) Viral Neuraminidase

**Correct Answer:** C) Viral Thymidine Kinase
**Explanation:** Acyclovir is brilliantly selective for infected cells. It only undergoes its initial phosphorylation (activation) when catalyzed by the enzyme Viral Thymidine Kinase, which is only present in cells actively infected by herpesviruses. Human cellular kinases cannot efficiently phosphorylate the initial acyclovir molecule. Once activated into a triphosphate form, it acts as a DNA chain terminator, specifically jamming the viral DNA polymerase and halting replication.

### Q7. Aminoglycoside antibiotics (like Gentamicin and Amikacin) are notorious for causing ototoxicity and nephrotoxicity. However, they are also strictly contraindicated in patients suffering from Myasthenia Gravis because they can trigger:
A) A severe extrapyramidal crisis
B) Life-threatening neuromuscular blockade
C) Massive histamine release
D) Spontaneous tendon rupture

**Correct Answer:** B) Life-threatening neuromuscular blockade
**Explanation:** Aminoglycosides possess a dangerous, curare-like effect at the neuromuscular junction. At high doses, or in vulnerable patients, they inhibit the pre-synaptic release of acetylcholine and decrease post-synaptic sensitivity. In a healthy person, this is rarely noticed. But in a patient with Myasthenia Gravis—who already suffers from a severe deficit of acetylcholine receptors—administering an aminoglycoside can trigger an immediate, fatal myasthenic crisis, paralyzing the respiratory muscles.

### Q8. In the event of a severe, intentional overdose of Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs) like Amitriptyline, the patient is at extreme risk of fatal cardiac arrhythmias (evidenced by a widened QRS complex on an ECG). Which specific intravenous antidote is administered to reverse this cardiac toxicity?
A) Flumazenil
B) Sodium Bicarbonate
C) Protamine Sulfate
D) Atropine

**Correct Answer:** B) Sodium Bicarbonate
**Explanation:** TCAs are highly cardiotoxic in overdose because they block fast sodium channels in the myocardium, profoundly slowing cardiac conduction (widened QRS) and causing lethal arrhythmias. Intravenous Sodium Bicarbonate is the gold-standard antidote. It provides a massive sodium load to overcome the channel blockade and alkalinizes the blood serum, which increases the non-ionized (protein-bound) fraction of the TCA, rendering it pharmacologically inactive and protecting the heart.

### Q9. While ACE inhibitors and ARBs are widely known, Aliskiren represents a different class of antihypertensives operating in the exact same physiological system. Aliskiren exerts its clinical effect as a:
A) Direct Renin Inhibitor
B) Aldosterone Receptor Antagonist
C) Angiotensin II Receptor Blocker
D) Potassium Channel Activator

**Correct Answer:** A) Direct Renin Inhibitor
**Explanation:** Aliskiren is the first and only orally active Direct Renin Inhibitor (DRI) approved for clinical use. It sits at the very top of the Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS) cascade. By binding tightly to the enzyme renin, it completely prevents the conversion of angiotensinogen into Angiotensin I. This shuts down the entire cascade, effectively lowering blood pressure. Like ACEs and ARBs, it is strictly contraindicated in pregnancy.

### Q10. A patient presents to the emergency department in a state of severe Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) with a blood glucose level of 600 mg/dL. Which specific formulation of insulin is the only one permitted to be administered intravenously (IV) to rapidly manage this crisis?
A) NPH Insulin (Isophane)
B) Insulin Glargine
C) Regular (Short-acting) Insulin
D) Insulin Detemir

**Correct Answer:** C) Regular (Short-acting) Insulin
**Explanation:** Regular Insulin (unmodified, short-acting crystalline zinc insulin) is a clear solution and is the *only* type of insulin that can be safely administered directly into a vein (IV). In medical emergencies like Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) or Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS), IV regular insulin is given as a continuous infusion to rapidly bring down catastrophic blood glucose levels. All other long-acting or intermediate suspensions (NPH, Glargine, Lente) must be injected Subcutaneously (SC); injecting them IV would be fatal.

## Section 2: Pharmaceutics & Engineering

### Q11. Bentonite is a naturally occurring colloidal hydrated aluminum silicate widely used in pharmaceutical suspensions. It is highly valued by formulation scientists because it exhibits a specific rheological property called:
A) Dilatancy (Shear-thickening)
B) Rheopexy
C) Thixotropy
D) Newtonian flow

**Correct Answer:** C) Thixotropy
**Explanation:** Bentonite magma (a 5% w/v dispersion of bentonite in water) is a classic example of a thixotropic system. When resting on the shelf, the bentonite particles build a 3D "house of cards" structure, forming a rigid gel that perfectly suspends heavy drug particles. When shaken, the shear stress breaks the structure, turning it into a highly fluid liquid for pouring. Once the shaking stops, the gel slowly reforms.

### Q12. During high-speed tablet compression, if small fragments of the tablet's surface adhere to the face of the upper or lower punches, resulting in a pitted or highly defective tablet surface, this specific manufacturing defect is termed:
A) Capping
B) Lamination
C) Picking
D) Mottling

**Correct Answer:** C) Picking
**Explanation:** "Picking" occurs when a small amount of the granular material physically sticks to the face of the punch, creating a small pit or crater on the tablet's surface. It is especially common if the punch has deep engravings or logos (the material gets packed into the letters). It is primarily caused by excessive moisture in the granules or insufficient use of a hydrophobic lubricant (like Magnesium Stearate). "Sticking" is a broader term where material sticks to the die walls.

### Q13. In the science of pharmaceutical sterilization, the "D-value" (Decimal Reduction Time) is a critical biological parameter. What does the D-value mathematically represent?
A) The exact temperature required to kill all microorganisms in 10 minutes
B) The time required at a specific temperature to reduce the microbial population by 90% (one log cycle)
C) The amount of radiation absorbed by the packaging material
D) The time required to eliminate pyrogens from a solution

**Correct Answer:** B) The time required at a specific temperature to reduce the microbial population by 90% (one log cycle)
**Explanation:** The D-value (Decimal reduction time) is the precise time (in minutes) required at a given, constant temperature to destroy 90% of the viable microorganisms or spores present in a sample. This represents a 1-log reduction in the microbial count (e.g., dropping from 10,000 viable cells to 1,000 cells). It is the foundational calculation used to validate thermal sterilization processes like autoclaving to guarantee absolute patient safety.

### Q14. The Indian Pharmacopoeia classifies glass containers for injectables into specific types. Which official test is explicitly mandated to evaluate the chemical resistance and alkali leaching of Type II (Treated Soda-Lime) glass?
A) The Powdered Glass Test
B) The Water Attack Test
C) The Thermal Shock Test
D) The Karl Fischer Test

**Correct Answer:** B) The Water Attack Test
**Explanation:** Type II glass is regular soda-lime glass that has been internally treated (usually with sulfur dioxide fumes) to create a highly resistant inner surface, preventing alkali from leaching into the drug. Because only the *inner surface* is resistant, it must be tested using the Water Attack Test (filling the whole container with water and autoclaving it). If you crushed it up, you would expose the untreated inner core. Type I and Type III glasses are evaluated using the Powdered Glass Test.

### Q15. In a Metered Dose Inhaler (MDI) utilized for delivering asthma medications, which highly engineered mechanical component is strictly responsible for guaranteeing that exactly the same dose of drug is released with every single actuation?
A) The Actuator
B) The Dip Tube
C) The Metering Chamber (Metering Valve)
D) The Propellant Canister

**Correct Answer:** C) The Metering Chamber (Metering Valve)
**Explanation:** The metering valve is the most critical and complex component of an MDI. It contains a tiny, precisely machined internal cavity known as the metering chamber (typically holding between 25 to 100 microliters). When the canister is inverted and the valve is depressed, the chamber seals off from the rest of the bulk liquid in the can and empties only the exact volume contained within the chamber, guaranteeing accurate, highly reproducible dosing.

### Q16. According to Stokes’ Law, which governs the physical stability and sedimentation rate of pharmaceutical suspensions and emulsions, which modification will most effectively *decrease* the rate of creaming in an emulsion?
A) Increasing the density difference between the oil and water phases
B) Drastically reducing the droplet size of the dispersed phase via homogenization
C) Decreasing the viscosity of the continuous aqueous phase
D) Increasing the physical temperature of the formulation

**Correct Answer:** B) Drastically reducing the droplet size of the dispersed phase via homogenization
**Explanation:** Stokes' Law states that the rate of sedimentation or creaming (V) is directly proportional to the *square of the radius* (r²) of the droplet. Therefore, physically reducing the droplet size (radius) has an exponential effect on slowing down creaming. By passing the emulsion through a high-pressure homogenizer, the massive oil droplets are smashed into micro-droplets, creating a highly stable emulsion that will not separate on the shelf.

## Section 3: Pharmacognosy

### Q17. In the qualitative phytochemical screening of crude drugs, Wagner's reagent is a classic standard utilized to detect the presence of alkaloids. What is the exact chemical composition of Wagner's reagent?
A) Potassium bismuth iodide
B) Potassium mercuric iodide
C) Iodine and Potassium iodide in water
D) Saturated solution of picric acid

**Correct Answer:** C) Iodine and Potassium iodide in water
**Explanation:** Alkaloidal precipitation tests are high-yield exam topics. 
*   **Wagner's Reagent:** Iodine + Potassium iodide (yields a reddish-brown precipitate).
*   **Mayer's Reagent:** Potassium mercuric iodide (yields a cream/white precipitate).
*   **Dragendorff's Reagent:** Potassium bismuth iodide (yields an orange/red-brown precipitate).
*   **Hager's Reagent:** Saturated picric acid (yields a yellow precipitate).

### Q18. In the microscopic evaluation of powdered crude drugs, stomatal characteristics are vital for identification. Which highly specific type of stomata is diagnostically characteristic of Datura stramonium leaves?
A) Paracytic (Parallel-celled)
B) Diacytic (Cross-celled)
C) Anisocytic (Unequal-celled)
D) Anomocytic (Irregular-celled)

**Correct Answer:** C) Anisocytic (Unequal-celled)
**Explanation:** Datura stramonium (family Solanaceae) features Anisocytic stomata. In this distinct arrangement, the stomatal pore is surrounded by exactly three subsidiary epidermal cells, one of which is significantly and distinctly smaller than the other two. Senna leaves have Paracytic stomata, Peppermint has Diacytic stomata, and Digitalis has Anomocytic stomata. Microscopic evaluation prevents the adulteration of expensive crude drugs.

### Q19. Nux Vomica is a highly potent, toxic crude drug that acts as a powerful central nervous system stimulant. What is its exact biological source according to pharmacopeial monographs?
A) Dried ripe seeds of Strychnos nux-vomica
B) Dried bark of Strychnos nux-vomica
C) Dried leaves of Atropa belladonna
D) Dried rhizomes of Podophyllum hexandrum

**Correct Answer:** A) Dried ripe seeds of Strychnos nux-vomica
**Explanation:** Nux Vomica consists strictly of the dried, ripe seeds of the tree Strychnos nux-vomica (family Loganiaceae). These disc-shaped, highly poisonous seeds contain the powerful indole alkaloids Strychnine and Brucine. Strychnine acts as a deadly competitive antagonist at inhibitory glycine receptors in the spinal cord, causing horrific, tetanic muscle spasms and respiratory failure in the event of an overdose. 

### Q20. According to the foundational "Isoprene Rule" of phytochemistry, all naturally occurring Terpenoids are built by the condensation of repeating isoprene units. A "Monoterpene" molecule (such as Menthol or Camphor) is built from exactly how many isoprene units, and thus contains how many carbon atoms?
A) 1 isoprene unit (5 Carbon atoms)
B) 2 isoprene units (10 Carbon atoms)
C) 3 isoprene units (15 Carbon atoms)
D) 4 isoprene units (20 Carbon atoms)

**Correct Answer:** B) 2 isoprene units (10 Carbon atoms)
**Explanation:** The fundamental building block of all terpenoids is the 5-carbon Isoprene unit (C5H8). 
*   **Monoterpenes:** Formed by joining 2 isoprene units = 10 Carbon atoms (e.g., Menthol, Limonene).
*   **Sesquiterpenes:** Formed by 3 units = 15 Carbon atoms (e.g., Artemisinin).
*   **Diterpenes:** Formed by 4 units = 20 Carbon atoms (e.g., Taxol, Vitamin A).
*   **Triterpenes:** Formed by 6 units = 30 Carbon atoms (e.g., Squalene, Saponins).

### Q21. Cardamom, known as the "Queen of Spices," is widely used as a carminative and flavoring agent in pharmacy. It is biologically derived from Elettaria cardamomum. To which botanical family does this plant belong?
A) Umbelliferae
B) Myrtaceae
C) Zingiberaceae
D) Lauraceae

**Correct Answer:** C) Zingiberaceae
**Explanation:** Cardamom (Elettaria cardamomum) belongs to the family Zingiberaceae (the same botanical family as Ginger and Turmeric). The official medicinal and culinary part of the plant is the dried, fully ripe fruit (capsule) and its highly aromatic inner seeds, which are extremely rich in volatile oils like cineole and terpinyl acetate.

## Section 4: Jurisprudence, Biochemistry & Anatomy

### Q22. In response to the alarming rise of antibiotic resistance globally, the Government of India amended the Drugs and Cosmetics Rules to introduce a new Schedule. Which Schedule strictly dictates that certain advanced antibiotics and habit-forming drugs must be sold in a separate register, and the medicine box must carry a red warning label?
A) Schedule X
B) Schedule H1
C) Schedule G
D) Schedule C

**Correct Answer:** B) Schedule H1
**Explanation:** Schedule H1 was introduced in 2013 specifically to curb the rampant, over-the-counter abuse of 3rd and 4th generation antibiotics, anti-tuberculosis drugs, and certain habit-forming psychotropics. A retail pharmacist must legally maintain a separate register detailing the patient's name, doctor's name, and quantity dispensed for these drugs, and retain this record for three years. The drug package must prominently feature the "Rx" symbol in red.

### Q23. Before independence, the Indian pharmaceutical sector was flooded with adulterated and substandard drugs. To investigate this massive health crisis, the British Government formed the "Drugs Enquiry Committee" in 1930. Who was the famous Chairman of this pivotal committee?
A) Prof. M.L. Schroff
B) Dr. B.C. Roy
C) Col. R.N. Chopra
D) Acharya Prafulla Chandra Ray

**Correct Answer:** C) Col. R.N. Chopra
**Explanation:** Col. Ram Nath Chopra (R.N. Chopra) chaired the legendary Drugs Enquiry Committee (DEC) of 1930, widely known in history as the Chopra Committee. His groundbreaking 1931 report highlighted the disastrous lack of pharmacy regulation in India and directly recommended the urgent formation of a Central Pharmacy Council, the establishment of provincial drug laboratories, and the drafting of the monumental Drugs and Cosmetics Act of 1940.

### Q24. Nitric Oxide (NO) is a highly potent, short-lived endogenous vasodilator crucial for regulating blood pressure and vascular tone. In human biochemistry, which specific essential amino acid serves as the direct physiological precursor for the synthesis of Nitric Oxide?
A) Tryptophan
B) Tyrosine
C) L-Arginine
D) Histidine

**Correct Answer:** C) L-Arginine
**Explanation:** The enzyme Endothelial Nitric Oxide Synthase (eNOS) directly acts upon the amino acid L-Arginine, converting it into L-Citrulline and simultaneously releasing a free radical molecule of Nitric Oxide (NO). This NO rapidly diffuses into adjacent vascular smooth muscle, activating guanylyl cyclase, increasing cGMP, and causing intense, rapid vasodilation. Tryptophan is the precursor for Serotonin; Tyrosine is for Dopamine/Adrenaline.

### Q25. Aminotransferases (like AST and ALT), which are absolutely vital for amino acid metabolism and frequently measured in liver function tests, strictly require the presence of a specific vitamin coenzyme to function. Which vitamin provides this coenzyme?
A) Vitamin B1 (Thiamine)
B) Vitamin B3 (Niacin)
C) Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine)
D) Vitamin B12 (Cyanocobalamin)

**Correct Answer:** C) Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine)
**Explanation:** All transamination reactions in the human body—which transfer an amino group from an amino acid to a keto acid—are absolutely dependent on Pyridoxal Phosphate (PLP). PLP is the biologically active coenzyme form of Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine). A severe deficiency of B6 will immediately halt amino acid metabolism and the synthesis of critical neurotransmitters like GABA, leading to severe seizures. 

### Q26. In the human hepatobiliary system, Cholesterol is the fundamental, primary precursor for the endogenous biosynthesis of which of the following digestive compounds?
A) Pancreatic amylase
B) Bile acids (Cholic acid and Chenodeoxycholic acid)
C) Pepsinogen
D) Bilirubin

**Correct Answer:** B) Bile acids (Cholic acid and Chenodeoxycholic acid)
**Explanation:** The human liver constantly converts excess hydrophobic cholesterol into highly amphipathic (detergent-like) Bile Acids, primarily Cholic acid and Chenodeoxycholic acid. These are secreted into the gallbladder, where they are concentrated into bile. Upon eating a fatty meal, bile is released into the duodenum to aggressively emulsify dietary dietary fats and fat-soluble vitamins, making them accessible to pancreatic lipases for digestion.

### Q27. The human cardiovascular system is vast. Anatomically, which of the following is definitively recognized as the longest individual vein in the entire human body?
A) The Inferior Vena Cava
B) The Femoral Vein
C) The Great Saphenous Vein
D) The Jugular Vein

**Correct Answer:** C) The Great Saphenous Vein
**Explanation:** The Great Saphenous Vein is a massive, superficial vein running continuously from the medial malleolus of the ankle, all the way up the medial aspect of the leg and thigh, completely draining into the femoral vein near the groin. Because of its incredible length, strength, and accessibility, cardiac surgeons frequently harvest segments of it to use as bypass grafts during Coronary Artery Bypass Grafting (CABG) surgery. 

### Q28. A patient presents with acute, unilateral facial paralysis, an inability to close their right eye, and a drooping right mouth corner (a condition universally diagnosed as Bell's Palsy). This condition is caused by the inflammation and severe compression of which specific Cranial Nerve?
A) Trigeminal Nerve (CN V)
B) Facial Nerve (CN VII)
C) Glossopharyngeal Nerve (CN IX)
D) Vagus Nerve (CN X)

**Correct Answer:** B) Facial Nerve (CN VII)
**Explanation:** The Facial Nerve (Cranial Nerve VII) provides the absolute primary motor innervation to all the intricate muscles of facial expression. When this nerve is acutely inflamed (often post-viral infection), it swells and gets compressed within the narrow bony facial canal of the skull. This results in Bell's Palsy, causing a terrifying (but usually temporary) complete paralysis of one entire side of the face. 

### Q29. Malaria remains a catastrophic global health crisis. The protozoan parasite Plasmodium falciparum is the causative agent, but it must be transmitted by a specific vector. Which biological vector transmits Malaria to humans?
A) The male Aedes aegypti mosquito
B) The female Anopheles mosquito
C) The Tsetse fly
D) The Sandfly

**Correct Answer:** B) The female Anopheles mosquito
**Explanation:** Malaria is exclusively transmitted by the bite of an infected *female* Anopheles mosquito. The female requires the rich proteins present in human blood to properly develop her eggs (the male mosquito solely feeds on plant nectar and cannot bite humans). The Aedes aegypti mosquito transmits Dengue, Zika, and Yellow Fever. The Tsetse fly transmits African Sleeping Sickness, and the Sandfly transmits Leishmaniasis. 

### Q30. In the highly complex, automated inventory management system of a modern Hospital Pharmacy, which logistical principle is strictly universally applied to minimize the financial loss caused by drug expiration?
A) LIFO (Last-In, First-Out)
B) FEFO (First-Expired, First-Out)
C) JIT (Just-In-Time)
D) VED (Vital, Essential, Desirable)

**Correct Answer:** B) FEFO (First-Expired, First-Out)
**Explanation:** In the pharmaceutical sector, physical expiration dates dictate everything. Therefore, the FEFO (First-Expired, First-Out) principle is strictly enforced globally. This logistical rule mandates that the drugs with the absolute closest expiration date are always picked and dispensed to the patient *first*, completely regardless of when they physically arrived in the warehouse. This ensures massive, expensive drug batches don't rot on the back of the shelf.

## 🔥 Gold Standard Mnemonics and Tricks

Lock these into your brain. Examiners love these exact concepts.

### 1. Types of Hypersensitivity Reactions
**Mnemonic: A.C.I.D.**
*   **Type I:** **A**naphylactic (IgE mediated - e.g., Asthma, Bee sting, Penicillin allergy)
*   **Type II:** **C**ytotoxic (IgG/IgM mediated - e.g., Hemolytic anemia, Blood transfusion reactions)
*   **Type III:** **I**mmune complex (Antigen-Antibody complexes lodge in tissue - e.g., Rheumatoid arthritis, Serum sickness)
*   **Type IV:** **D**elayed (T-cell mediated, no antibodies involved - e.g., Tuberculosis Mantoux test, Contact dermatitis)

### 2. Cytochrome P450 Enzyme Inhibitors
**Mnemonic: SICKFACES.COM**
These drugs slow down the liver, causing other drugs to accumulate and become highly toxic!
*   **S**odium Valproate
*   **I**soniazid
*   **C**imetidine
*   **K**etoconazole
*   **F**luconazole
*   **A**lcohol (Acute binge drinking)
*   **C**hloramphenicol
*   **E**rythromycin (Macrolides)
*   **S**ulfonamides
*   **C**iprofloxacin
*   **O**meprazole
*   **M**etronidazole / Macrolides

### 3. Diuretic Sites of Action in the Kidney
**Mnemonic: COLT (from proximal to distal)**
*   **C**arbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors (Acetazolamide) $\rightarrow$ **Proximal Convoluted Tubule**
*   **O**smotic Diuretics (Mannitol) $\rightarrow$ **Descending Loop of Henle**
*   **L**oop Diuretics (Furosemide) $\rightarrow$ **Thick Ascending Limb of Loop of Henle**
*   **T**hiazides (Hydrochlorothiazide) $\rightarrow$ **Distal Convoluted Tubule**
